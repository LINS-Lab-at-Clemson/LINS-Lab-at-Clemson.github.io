abstract = """The rise in smart water technologies has introduced new cybersecurity vulnerabilities for water infrastructures. However, the implications of cyber-physical attacks on systems like urban drainage systems remain underexplored. This research addresses this gap by introducing a method to quantify flood risks in the face of cyber-physical threats. We apply this approach to a smart stormwater system—a real-time controlled network of pond-conduit configurations fitted with water-level detectors and gate regulators. Our focus is on false data injection attacks, in which adversaries introduce deceptive data that mimics legitimate system noise and evades detection. Our risk assessment incorporates factors such as sensor noise and weather-prediction uncertainty. Findings reveal that false data injection can amplify flood risks by feeding the control system false data, leading to erroneous outflow directives. Notably, these attacks can reshape flood-risk dynamics across different storm intensities, accentuating risks during less severe but more frequent storms. This study offers insights for investments in smart stormwater systems and cyber-defense planning, and the risk-quantification method can be extended to irrigation channels, multi-reservoir systems, and other water-system networks."""
